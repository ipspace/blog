    I recall something similar happening with Arista switches at some point, not with LLDP but one of the other slow protocols (can't remember which one).

    Without going into standards (nobody reads them anyway), the way it's usually implemented in a switch is that there is a ACL with a list of special MAC addresses that the switch should capture for further processing and not just flood. If the special MAC address for a given protocol is not on this list, it's treated as a regular Ethernet frame, i.e. flooded.

    But what if this switch does not claim compliance to MEF 45.1?

    Service providers might manage a CE L2 device in a way that it should be invisible to the customer. Then of course LLDP flooding looks logical. Topology hiding is a basic requirement for service provider networks. It could be applied to L2 services, too.

    If the customer manages the CE, then normally it would not happen.

    It all depends on your concept of how you define your WAN services.

    The main issue is thinking in ISO reference model terms. You should think in G.805/G.809 terms when you cover service provider networks. Then it is easy to understand why LLDP flooding might be the preferred way...
