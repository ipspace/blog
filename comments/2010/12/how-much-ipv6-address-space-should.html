  13. Ben Jencks 12 December 2010 10:18
    /64 is generally accepted as a bad idea (except for 6rd, where a /32 plus 32 bits of v4 address give a /64, but that's a special case for transition technology). /56 and /48 are both reasonable, but there is a reason you might want a /48 even if you're not likely to have >256 networks in a home: easy automated hierarchical delegation in a routed home. If you delegate a /48 to the ISP-facing CPE, it can then delegate (for instance) a /56 to each routing device connected to it, and each of those devices can have multiple interfaces and possibly more devices behind them.

    The 8 bit boundary (/48 to /56) is arbitrary, it could as easily be 4, 5, or 6 bits. The point is that with 16 bits to work with, you can have a lot of flexibility in delegation, allowing for both plenty of levels of depth and plenty of devices at each level, but with only 8 bits those tradeoffs become much more acute. So, without a pressing reason to assign less than a /48, just use /48.

  35. PauloJ 28 October 2015 11:54
    A lot of this depends on whether we will retain the idea of PA and PI space. Most residential users wouldn't need more than a /96 (giving them a personal allocation equivalent to the entire IPv4 address space), however we don't want Internet routers to have to learn routes to every single endpoint customer in the world (as well as the 500k IPv4 tables it'll likely be dual stacking with). If we retain the idea of PA (Provider Aggregated) space, then the wider internet will only have 1 route to the /29 for that ISP, internally they may subnet that down to hundreds of millions of customer ranges without impacting routing tables of their peers. The size of those end user networks would then be driven by their internal routing capabilities and customer requirements.
    A uniquely advertised prefix is only truly necessary if it is going to be dual homed to more than one provider.
